COVID-19: What you should know Below, some important information and facts about the corona virus. The information comes from the World Health Organization (WHO) sources.

COVID-19 is the disease caused by a novel coronavirus, SARS-CoV-2. WHO learned of this new virus on December 31, 2019, when an outbreak of "viral pneumonia" cases was reported in Wuhan, People's Republic of China.

The most common symptoms are:
  • Fever
  • Dry cough
  • Fatigue
Other less common symptoms may affect some patients:
  • Loss of taste and smell
  • Nasal congestion
  • Conjunctivitis (red eyes)
  • Sore throat
  • Headaches
  • Muscle or joint pain
  • Various types of skin rashes
  • Nausea or vomiting
  • Diarrhea
  • Chills or dizziness
Symptoms of the severe form of COVID-19 include:
  • Shortness of breath
  • Loss of appetite
  • Confusional state
  • Persistent chest pain or tightness
  • High temperature (over 38Â° C).
Other symptoms are less common:
  • Irritability
  • Confusional state
  • Altered consciousness (sometimes associated with seizures)
  • Anxiety disorders
  • Depression
  • Sleep disturbances

Of those patients who develop symptoms, most (about 80%) recover without the need for hospitalization. About 15% of patients become critically ill and require oxygen therapy, and 5% of infections are critical and require intensive care... Complications leading to death include respiratory failure, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), sepsis and septic shock, thromboembolism, and/or multivisceral failure, causing damage to the heart, liver, or kidneys. In rare cases, children may develop a severe inflammatory syndrome within weeks of infection.

For your safety, take a few simple precautions: maintain physical separation, wear a mask (especially when separation is not possible), ensure proper ventilation of rooms, avoid crowds and close contact, wash your hands regularly, and cough into your elbow or into a tissue. Follow local guidelines where you live and work. Take all these precautions!

Anyone with symptoms should be tested, if possible. People who do not have symptoms, but who have been in close contact with an infected or potentially infected person, may also want to consider getting tested - check and follow local health guidelines. While a person is waiting for test results, they should remain isolated from others. When testing capabilities are limited, priority should be given to testing those at greatest risk of infection, such as health care workers, and those most likely to develop a severe form of the disease, such as the elderly, especially those living in nursing homes or long-term care facilities.

In most cases, a molecular test is used to screen for SARS-CoV-2 and confirm infection. The most commonly used molecular test is the polymerase chain reaction (PCR) test. A swab is taken from the nose and/or throat. The molecular test detects the presence of the virus in the swab by amplifying the viral genetic material to detectable levels. Therefore, a molecular test is used to confirm an active infection, usually within a few days of exposure and around the time that symptoms may appear. Rapid diagnostic antigen tests (also called rapid diagnostic tests or RDTs) detect viral proteins (known as antigens). Samples are taken from the nose and/or throat using a swab. These tests are cheaper than PCR tests and give a result more quickly, but they are generally less reliable. These tests are most effective when the virus is circulating widely in a community and when the sample is taken when the person is most contagious.

Large efficacy and safety studies of five vaccine candidates, including the three in question (the other two being Moderna and AstraZeneca), have been made public via press release, but only one company (AstraZeneca) has published results in a peer-reviewed journal.
